# Chapter 6

## The God of Control

### The Portrait

The God of Control emerges wherever human beings long for assurance that reality is not chaos. He is the God imagined by hearts that cannot bear the thought that life might be random, history might be drifting, or suffering might fall outside all meaning. Through this lens, God appears as the great orchestrator of all things: the one who governs, arranges, permits, restrains, and directs the movement of the world according to a wisdom far greater than our own.

This God does not merely react to events as they unfold. He is not improvising his way through history. Nothing catches him off guard. Nothing enters the story outside his awareness. He sees the whole pattern at once, from beginning to end, and what appears to us as interruption, accident, or disaster is held within a design we cannot yet perceive. He is the architect behind the structure, the strategist behind the movement, the ruler whose purposes remain steady even when the world beneath them trembles.

To imagine God this way is to imagine a universe that is fundamentally ordered. Beneath the surface confusion of ordinary life there is still a hidden coherence. Beneath loss, delay, disappointment, and disruption there remains a will that has not lost hold of the world. The God of Control is therefore not only a figure of authority but a figure of reassurance. He promises that history is not spinning loose, that pain is not meaningless, and that even what appears senseless may yet belong to a larger purpose.

For this reason, the God of Control often appears most vividly in seasons when life feels unmanageable. When illness enters the home, when the future grows uncertain, when relationships fracture, when the mind itself becomes unstable, the soul longs to know that someone wiser is still holding the story together. The God of Control answers that longing by offering a vision of reality in which nothing is wasted, nothing is purely accidental, and nothing finally escapes the reach of providence.

This image of God has endured because it speaks to one of the deepest human needs: the need to believe that the world is held. It offers more than comfort. It offers structure. It tells us that beneath the unpredictability of life there is still intelligence, intention, and care. It assures us that we do not stand alone before the chaos, and that what we cannot govern may yet be governed by One who can.

Seen through this lens, God is the keeper of order, the sovereign of outcomes, the hidden hand beneath events. He is the one whose plans cannot be thwarted, whose purposes are larger than our fears, and whose control extends even into places we cannot understand. He is the God of providence, design, and unseen governance—the God who reassures anxious hearts that the universe has not slipped from its axis.

### The Worship

To worship the God of Control is to live as though life is not meant to be drifted through, but stewarded. If God governs the world with wisdom, order, and purpose, then the faithful response is not carelessness but attentiveness. One does not simply throw up their hands and call it trust. One studies, plans, prepares, discerns, organizes, and labors to bring one's own life into alignment with the deeper order of things. Worship, under this image, is not limited to prayer or doctrine. It becomes a way of inhabiting the world—carefully, responsibly, and with an eye toward consequences.

At its best, this form of worship is rooted in stewardship. The disciple of the God of Control believes that human beings have been entrusted with minds to think, hands to build, and responsibilities to bear. Prudence becomes a virtue. So does discipline. One learns to anticipate what may be coming, to prepare for hardship, to make wise decisions, and to avoid unnecessary chaos where it can be avoided. Life is approached with seriousness. Choices matter. Habits matter. Structures matter. The future is shaped, at least in part, by what one does in the present, and so worship takes the form of diligent participation in the ordering of one's life.

This can produce much that is good. It can create people who are dependable, conscientious, and thoughtful. People who plan ahead, care for others, pay attention to details, keep their promises, and try to use their gifts well. It resists passivity and fatalism. It refuses to treat "God is in control" as an excuse for laziness or indifference. Instead, it assumes that divine sovereignty calls forth human responsibility. If God has given us a mind, then we are meant to use it. If God has entrusted us with work, relationships, and decisions, then we are meant to tend them with care.

Yet the worship of the God of Control rarely remains this simple.

For beneath the diligence there often lies a quieter desire: not merely to be faithful within God's world, but to help ensure that God's world unfolds correctly. The line between stewardship and management can be thin. Planning begins as wisdom, but it can slowly become an attempt to secure the future against uncertainty. Prayer begins as trust, but it can become a search for reassurance that the right outcome will occur. Discernment begins as attentiveness to God, but it can harden into hypervigilance, the exhausting effort to interpret every event, avoid every mistake, and stay one step ahead of whatever might go wrong.

In this way, worship of the God of Control often takes on two forms at once.

The first is genuine surrender. It is the act of placing what we cannot carry into the hands of One who can. It is the prayer whispered in hospital rooms, over wayward children, beside financial uncertainty, and in the long hours of inner turmoil: *I cannot hold this together. You must hold it for me.* This is one of the deepest gifts of this image. It gives the anxious heart somewhere to rest. It allows the overwhelmed soul to believe that what exceeds its strength does not exceed God's. In this mode, worship becomes relinquishment. It opens the clenched fist. It releases outcomes into a providence larger than human foresight.

But the second form of worship is more complicated. It is the attempt to assist God in managing the outcome. The worshipper does not simply ask for wisdom; they feel pressure to get the plan right. They do not merely seek to be faithful; they begin to feel responsible for how everything turns out. They search for the hidden lesson in every setback, the correct decision behind every fork in the road, the right formula that will keep life from unraveling. If they pray hard enough, plan carefully enough, think clearly enough, obey thoroughly enough, perhaps disaster can be prevented. Perhaps the future can be stabilized. Perhaps uncertainty can be tamed.

This impulse is rarely born from arrogance alone. More often it grows out of love, fear, and responsibility. It comes from not wanting to fail the people one cares for. It comes from having lived through enough chaos to know how painful disorder can be. It comes from the quiet terror that if one loosens their grip, life may fall apart. The disciple of the God of Control may genuinely long to trust, but trust is difficult when vigilance has long felt like the only thing keeping disaster at bay.

And so worship becomes a strange mixture of prayer and management. The person asks God to take control, yet keeps checking the locks. They ask for peace, yet continue running every scenario through their mind. They seek divine guidance, yet cannot stop trying to decipher the entire blueprint for themselves. They believe God is sovereign, but still live as though the future rests largely on their ability to anticipate, organize, and carry what has been placed before them.

This is where the burden of this worship begins to show. For when faithfulness becomes entangled with outcome, the soul grows tired. Rest becomes difficult. Uncertainty feels unbearable. Every decision acquires too much weight, every mistake too much significance, every delay too much meaning. The worshipper may still speak the language of trust, but inwardly they are carrying more than they were ever meant to carry. The God who was meant to relieve anxiety can, in practice, become the center of an anxious spirituality in which one is forever trying to discern, prevent, manage, and secure.

Yet even here, the God of Control may still have something to teach.

For if the early movement of this worship is stewardship, its mature form is surrender. Not passivity. Not carelessness. Not the refusal to use the gifts one has been given. Rather, it is the slow and painful recognition that responsibility is not the same thing as sovereignty. One may plan wisely without mastering the future. One may act faithfully without guaranteeing the outcome. One may use the mind God has given without turning that mind into a savior.

To worship the God of Control in its deepest form is to do what love and wisdom require, and then to open the hand. It is to labor diligently while admitting that the final shape of things does not belong to us. It is to participate in God's order without pretending to occupy God's throne. It is to discover, often against every anxious instinct, that dependence is not failure. Dependence is simply the truth.

And perhaps that is the hardest lesson this God has to teach: that peace does not come when everything is finally under our management, but when we learn, little by little, to stop confusing faithfulness with control.
