# Part I

## Images of Survival

Every spiritual life begins somewhere, and more often than not it begins close to the earth.

Before we ever asked what is beautiful, or what is true, or what love might ask of us, we asked a simpler and more urgent question: *How do we survive?* Before there were doctrines to defend or philosophies to refine, there were winter nights, empty fields, invading armies, wild animals, sickness in the body, and the terrible knowledge that everything we love can be taken from us. Human beings did not first search for God from a place of safety. We searched from the edge of vulnerability. We searched while hungry, afraid, exposed, and unsure whether tomorrow would come.

It should not surprise us, then, that some of our oldest and most powerful images of God are images born in that atmosphere. When life feels fragile, God is often imagined in ways that can hold back the dark. God appears as the one who punishes what threatens the community, who draws a boundary around a people, who raises up strong rulers, who defends order against chaos, who rewards loyalty and warns against betrayal. In these portraits, God is not first encountered as mystery, or tenderness, or beauty, but as shelter, authority, judgment, and strength. The divine comes clothed in the garments of survival.

This does not make these images false. It makes them human.

They arise from the oldest layers of the soul, from the part of us that knows life can break, that children must be protected, that communities can dissolve, that danger is not imaginary. There is a reason such images endure. They speak to something elemental in us. They know that before a person can contemplate transcendence, he must first make it through the night. Before a people can speak of freedom, they must survive conquest. Before the heart can open to mystery, it often longs for walls, for shelter, for some assurance that the world is not entirely wild.

So Part I begins here, with what might be called "the images of survival": the Wrathful God, the Tribal God, and the King God. These are not separate deities competing for allegiance, but recurring ways human beings have imagined the one God when life is felt most urgently as threat, struggle, and the need for preservation. They are images forged where fear and faith meet—where the human longing for God is braided together with the human longing to be safe.

And to be fair to these images, we must begin by acknowledging the truth they carry.

They know that evil is real. They know that communities require order if they are to endure. They know that loyalty matters, that a people without memory dissolves, that a life without structure drifts. They know that there are moments when gentleness alone does not seem sufficient to hold back the flood. They remind us that spiritual life is not lived by disembodied souls floating above history, but by vulnerable creatures of flesh and blood—creatures who must eat, defend their children, bury their dead, and somehow continue when the world around them is unstable.

There is wisdom in that. There is even mercy in it.

For what is a wrathful image of God if not an attempt to believe that injustice will not have the last word? What is a tribal image of God if not the hope that one’s people are not forgotten in the vastness of history? What is a kingly image of God if not the longing for a power strong enough to restrain chaos and hold the world together? These portraits endure because they answer real cries of the human heart. They offer seriousness in a dangerous world. They offer belonging in a lonely one. They offer order in a world that can so easily come apart.

And yet every image that protects us can also begin to imprison us if it is taken for the whole.

A vision of God born in fear may struggle to imagine mercy for the outsider. A portrait of God shaped by tribal loyalty may find it difficult to bless those beyond the boundary. An image of God formed around power and protection may teach reverence and obedience, yet leave little room for tenderness, freedom, or wonder. What once served survival can harden into suspicion. What once preserved a people can narrow the heart. The fortress that kept danger out can also keep love from getting in.

This is the tension we will carry through the chapters ahead. The images of survival are not wrong because they speak to fear. Fear is part of being human. The problem comes when fear is given the final word, when a true but partial vision of God becomes so dominant that everything else in the divine life is pushed to the edges. Then judgment eclipses compassion. Loyalty eclipses universality. Order eclipses freedom. Survival, which is a necessary beginning, quietly becomes the whole story.

But survival is never the whole story.

Still, we should not move too quickly past these images, as though the only mature spirituality is the one that has left them behind. Most of us have not. These portraits remain alive in our politics, our churches, our families, and our private anxieties. They rise in us whenever life feels threatened. We invoke them when we divide the world into insiders and outsiders, when we long for someone strong enough to restore order, when we fear that softness will get us killed, when we confuse vigilance with faithfulness. The ancient images still live in modern hearts.

So we begin here not to mock them, but to understand them. We begin here because the soul must tell the truth about what it fears, what it clings to, and what kind of God it reaches for when the lights go out. We begin here because even our holiest language is often woven together with our oldest survival instincts. And we begin here because if we are ever to grow into a wider, freer, more spacious love, we must first learn to recognize the images that taught us how to endure.

The chapters that follow will linger with three such images. Each reveals something real. Each protects something precious. Each also leaves something in shadow. And each, if worshiped long enough, forms a certain kind of soul.

For we become like what we worship.

And before we can speak of freedom, beauty, compassion, or love, we must first pass through the older sanctuaries—the places where human beings learned to bow before the God they hoped would keep them alive.
