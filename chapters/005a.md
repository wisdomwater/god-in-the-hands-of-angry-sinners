# Part II

## Images of Achievement

The images of survival ask a simple question:

*How do I stay alive?*

They emerge wherever life feels fragile and uncertain. When God is encountered primarily as protector, ruler, or guardian, the deepest concerns become safety, belonging, order, and stability.

Yet once life becomes secure enough to breathe, another question begins to stir.

It is no longer enough merely to survive.

Somewhere within the human heart another longing awakens.

*Who am I?*

Or perhaps more honestly:

*Who might I become?*

This longing gives rise to the images of achievement.

Here, God is encountered through the language of growth, responsibility, and possibility. The divine is seen as the one who invites us to cultivate our gifts, strengthen our character, and participate in the unfinished work of creation. Through discipline, wisdom, perseverance, and sacrifice, we become more than we are today.

This voice is familiar to nearly every human being.

*Work faithfully.*

*Learn continually.*

*Become stronger.*

*Reach beyond yourself.*

*Do not remain what you are.*

There is something deeply beautiful in this invitation. We admire the athlete who patiently trains, the artist devoted to beauty, the parent who sacrifices for a child, the entrepreneur who builds something new, and the seeker who gives themselves to spiritual transformation. Across cultures and centuries, such lives have captivated our imagination because they embody a hope we instinctively recognize: human beings can grow.

These images of God honor that capacity. They cultivate responsibility, resilience, courage, excellence, and initiative. They remind us that our choices matter, that effort bears fruit, and that our lives can become a blessing to others.

Yet beneath every achievement lies a deeper longing than accomplishment itself. We do not merely wish to *do* something.

We long to *be* someone.

We seek a life that is meaningful, an identity that can bear the weight of our hopes. In this sense, the images of achievement also shape the self. Through challenge, success, disappointment, and perseverance, we discover our strengths, our limitations, and our calling.

For this reason, the contemplative traditions are sometimes misunderstood. When mystics speak of transcending the ego, it can sound as though the self is something to be discarded. Yet a healthy self is rarely the enemy of spiritual growth. More often, it is one of its necessary foundations.

Before we can surrender ourselves, we must first possess a self to surrender. Before we can release our identity into God, we must first discover the person we have become. The story we tell about ourselves is not false simply because it is incomplete. It is a chapter, not the entire book.

Like every image of God explored in these pages, the images of achievement reveal something true while concealing something else. Their shadow appears when growth becomes ultimate. Vocation becomes ambition without rest. Identity becomes performance. Worth becomes productivity. Failure begins to feel like annihilation because the self has forgotten that it is loved apart from what it accomplishes.

These images offer a profound gift, but they cannot answer every question. They can teach us how to become someone, but they cannot finally tell us who we are when every accomplishment has faded and every title has been forgotten.

Even so, their place in the human journey is indispensable. Few people arrive at wisdom, compassion, or surrender without first passing through seasons of effort, ambition, discipline, and self-discovery. The desire to become is not opposed to the spiritual life. It is often one of the ways by which God first draws us forward.

The chapters that follow explore two powerful images of this journey.

The first encounters God through the lens of control—the one who calls us to master uncertainty through wisdom, planning, and discipline.

The second encounters God as the giver of success and fruitfulness, whose blessing appears in accomplishment, influence, and fulfilled potential.

Both preserve genuine wisdom.

Both cast long shadows when one facet of God is mistaken for the whole.

For we become like what we worship.

Before we can understand what these images are making of us, we must first understand why they continue to capture the human heart.
