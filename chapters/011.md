# Chapter 11

## The God of Beauty

### Unproductively Extravagant

In this image, God is encountered through beauty.

Not beauty as decoration or luxury, but as one of the deepest ways the divine makes itself known. Here God is not first experienced through commandments or arguments, nor through systems of thought or carefully constructed doctrines. God is encountered in the quiet astonishment that overtakes us when something beautiful arrests our attention and, if only for a moment, draws us beyond ourselves.

This portrait of God begins with a simple observation: beauty has a curious power over the human heart. It interrupts us. We do not command beauty to appear, nor can we manufacture the experience of awe by force of will. Beauty arrives as a gift. A sunrise spills across the horizon. A violin begins to sing. A child's laughter fills a room. Snow settles silently upon pine branches. The night sky opens above the desert in impossible abundance. Before we have time to analyze what is happening, something inside us has already begun to bow.

For a brief moment, the constant narration of the mind falls silent.

That silence may be one of beauty's greatest miracles.

So much of our lives is spent evaluating, comparing, planning, worrying, and calculating. Our attention becomes divided among responsibilities, ambitions, fears, and endless streams of information. Even our relationships can become transactional, subtly measured by what they provide or require. We learn to ask practical questions. Is this useful? Will it work? Is it worth the cost? What will I gain?

Beauty answers none of these questions.

Instead, it asks another.

Can you simply behold?

This image of God suggests that the deepest truths are not always argued into existence. Sometimes they are received with open hands. Sometimes reality discloses itself not through explanation but through wonder. The divine is encountered less like a problem to solve than like a landscape into which one slowly awakens.

In this vision, beauty is not an ornament added to an otherwise functional universe. It is woven into the very fabric of creation. The world contains an astonishing extravagance that serves no obvious practical purpose. Flowers bloom in forgotten meadows where no human eye may ever rest upon them. Birds pour out songs that far exceed the minimum needed to communicate. Snowflakes form intricate patterns that disappear moments after touching the earth. The rings of Saturn circle a planet that most of human history never knew existed. Nebulae blossom in distant galaxies beyond the reach of every telescope for countless generations.

Creation is astonishingly, almost scandalously, beautiful.

If efficiency alone governed reality, much of this would seem unnecessary. A world built only for function could have been simpler. Trees might all bear the same shape. Every flower could wear the same color. Mountains could be flattened into fields. Human faces could convey information without ever becoming beautiful. Music could be replaced by signals. Poetry by instruction manuals.

Instead, the universe overflows.

It is as though existence delights in exceeding necessity.

To those formed by this image, that abundance reveals something about the heart of God. The Creator is not merely an engineer solving problems or a ruler maintaining order. The Creator is also an artist, pouring generosity into every corner of existence. Beauty becomes a quiet testimony that reality is not only sustained by wisdom and power but also animated by delight. God creates not only what is needed, but what can be loved.

This image does not deny truth, nor does it diminish goodness. Rather, it sees beauty as their radiant companion. Truth tells us what is. Goodness teaches us how to live. Beauty awakens our desire for both. Before we choose the good or understand the true, we are often first drawn by what is beautiful. Beauty invites rather than compels. It woos rather than demands. It expands the heart before it persuades the mind.

Perhaps this is why beauty has so often served as a doorway into the spiritual life. Across cultures and centuries, people have found themselves unexpectedly overcome while standing before towering mountains, listening to sacred music, gazing upon great works of art, or watching light dance across the surface of the sea. Even those with little interest in religion instinctively reach for sacred language. They speak of transcendence, mystery, grace, or presence. Something greater than themselves seems to shimmer through ordinary things.

In this portrait, beauty becomes almost sacramental. It is never merely an object to possess or consume. It is a window through which the soul glimpses a reality larger than itself. The beautiful thing is not the destination; it is the invitation. Like an icon whose purpose is not to draw attention to wood and paint but to awaken the heart to the holy, every beautiful thing quietly points beyond itself. It whispers that the visible world is translucent with a deeper light.

Yet perhaps the greatest gift of this image is not that it teaches us to admire beautiful things, but that it teaches us to pay attention. Beauty cannot be hurried. It refuses to reveal itself to the distracted glance. It asks us to slow our pace, to linger, to notice the small and the ordinary. The steam rising from a morning cup of tea. The wrinkles around a grandmother's smile. The rhythm of rain against the window. The patient unfolding of spring after a long winter.

In a culture captivated by speed, productivity, and measurable outcomes, such attention can appear almost wasteful. To spend an afternoon watching waves break upon the shore accomplishes nothing. To sit quietly in a garden does not increase efficiency. To lose oneself in a symphony produces no tangible profit.

And yet perhaps this is precisely the point.

This image of God gently suggests that not everything precious exists to be useful. Some things exist simply to be received with gratitude. Some moments cannot be improved by accomplishing more. Some realities can only be known through wonder.

For when God is encountered as Beauty, the soul begins to discover that life is more than survival, achievement, or even understanding. It is, at its deepest, a gift to behold.

### A Window into the Infinite

When God is imagined primarily through the lens of beauty, worship begins not with obligation but with attention.

It is less concerned with mastering ideas than with learning to behold. The soul formed by this image discovers that there are moments when the deepest response to God is not another explanation, but a quiet silence. Beauty has interrupted the endless conversation of the mind. The need to evaluate, categorize, and possess falls away, replaced by a simple awareness that something greater is present.

This kind of worship is not confined to sanctuaries or liturgies. It unfolds wherever the ordinary becomes translucent with the extraordinary. A sunrise spilling over distant hills. The sound of rain against the window. A Bach prelude that seems to carry more meaning than words can hold. The laughter of children echoing through a park. A meal shared around a weathered table. A painting that leaves us unexpectedly still. Such moments are not merely appreciated; they are received as quiet invitations. Through them, the soul senses that reality is deeper, richer, and more radiant than it first appeared.

In this vision of God, beauty is never the destination. It is the doorway.

Every truly beautiful thing points beyond itself. A mountain does not ask to be worshiped. Neither does a symphony, a flower, nor a star-filled sky. Their gift is not that they capture our hearts forever, but that they awaken them. They stir a longing that no created thing can fully satisfy. They whisper of a fullness that lies beyond themselves. Beauty is sacramental in this way. It becomes a visible sign of an invisible mystery, revealing that the world is not closed in upon itself but open to transcendence.

This is why beauty has so often become the language of worship. Across every culture and generation, human beings have built temples that reached toward the heavens, composed music that seemed to stretch beyond language, painted icons whose purpose was not merely to be admired but to become windows into the holy. Even those who claim no religious faith often find themselves speaking in sacred language when beauty overwhelms them. They describe moments as transcendent, holy, or filled with grace. They instinctively sense that they have encountered something larger than themselves, even if they cannot name it.

Perhaps beauty succeeds where arguments cannot because it bypasses the restless machinery of thought. It does not first ask us to agree. It asks us to see.

Or perhaps more accurately, it asks us to see differently.

So much of life trains us to look through the world without truly noticing it. We hurry from task to task, evaluating everything by its usefulness. What can this accomplish? What problem does it solve? How can it make me more efficient? Even our relationships can become subtly transactional, measured by expectations and outcomes. Beauty quietly interrupts this way of seeing. It refuses to justify itself by productivity. It asks for nothing except our presence.

In that sense, worship shaped by beauty becomes an act of resistance.

It resists the temptation to reduce creation to raw material for our ambitions. It resists the impulse to hurry past the present in pursuit of the next achievement. It resists the belief that value can always be measured by usefulness or profit. To pause before a sunset, to lose oneself in a symphony, to linger in the stillness of a forest, or to gaze in wonder at the night sky accomplishes nothing that can be entered into a spreadsheet.

And yet it accomplishes something far deeper.

It reminds us that life is not merely a project to complete but a gift to receive.

This image of God forms worshipers who practice paying attention. They learn that attention itself can become a prayer. They notice the wrinkles around an aging face, the intricate veins of a single leaf, the changing colors of the evening sky, the quiet dignity of ordinary moments that others rush past. They become less interested in consuming experiences than in inhabiting them. The world slowly changes, not because creation itself has become more beautiful, but because their eyes have become more awake.

As this attentiveness deepens, something unexpected begins to happen. The beautiful object gradually becomes less important than the beauty to which it points. A magnificent cathedral eventually directs the heart beyond stone and stained glass. Music leaves behind a silence that somehow feels more alive than the notes themselves. The beauty of a beloved person awakens gratitude for the mystery of personhood itself. Every beautiful thing becomes a finger pointing toward the moon, inviting the soul beyond the gift toward the Giver, beyond the visible toward the invisible, beyond the finite toward the infinite.

This is perhaps the deepest movement of worship under this image of God.

It does not end in admiration but in communion.

The purpose of beauty is not to distract us from reality, nor even to entertain us. It is to awaken us. For a fleeting moment, the veil grows thin, and we glimpse a world that is more luminous than our ordinary habits of perception allow us to see. Heaven does not descend from some distant realm so much as reveal itself already shimmering within the ordinary. The holy was never absent. It was our attention that had wandered.

Those who worship through beauty therefore spend their lives returning to presence. They discover that the infinite is often tasted, not in dramatic visions, but in moments of unguarded wonder. They learn that there are realities too deep for argument and truths too beautiful for explanation. They begin to understand that while reason can describe the feast, beauty invites us to the table.

For when God is encountered as Beauty, worship becomes more than believing the right things about the divine.

It becomes learning, again and again, how to behold.

### Caves of Light

Every enduring image of God survives because it protects something essential. The image of God as Beauty preserves one of the deepest truths a human being can discover: we were not created merely to survive. We were created to wonder.

This is no small gift.

Much of life presses us toward necessity. We wake to alarms, fulfill obligations, solve problems, answer emails, meet deadlines, pay bills, care for families, and shoulder responsibilities that cannot be ignored. These things matter. They are part of what it means to live faithfully in the world. Yet if necessity becomes the whole story, something within us slowly begins to shrink. Life is reduced to a series of transactions, and we begin to measure ourselves—and everything around us—by usefulness alone.

How productive was today? What did I accomplish? What value did I create? What problem did I solve?

These questions are not wrong. They simply cannot bear the weight of a human life.

This image of God gently reminds us that there is another way of seeing. It invites us to remember that before we are workers, consumers, achievers, or even thinkers, we are creatures capable of wonder. We inhabit a world that continually offers itself to us as gift. The scent of rain drifting through an open window. The warmth of sunlight across an old wooden floor. The quiet dignity of an ancient oak. The laughter of children echoing through a neighborhood. The intricate geometry of a snowflake that will disappear within moments of touching the earth. None of these moments increase our wealth or advance our ambitions. Yet together they become the texture of a life worth living.

In this image, beauty becomes a quiet protest against the belief that value is measured only by utility.

Modern life often teaches us that flourishing comes through optimization. Every minute should become more productive. Every hobby should become a side business. Every skill should increase our value. Every relationship should expand our network. Every inconvenience should be eliminated, every idle moment filled. We have become remarkably efficient at improving the machinery of life.

And yet many of us feel strangely less alive.

We have learned how to save time without knowing what time is for.

Beauty interrupts that endless striving. It reminds us that not everything precious must justify its existence through usefulness. Some realities are valuable simply because they are beautiful. Some moments exist not to produce something else but to be received with gratitude. A sunset asks for nothing. Music demands no measurable return. A flower blooms whether anyone notices it or not. Their beauty is gloriously extravagant.

Perhaps that extravagance reveals something about the heart of God.

The universe seems almost impossibly generous. Flowers bloom in forgotten valleys where no human foot may ever tread. Coral reefs shimmer beneath oceans unseen by countless generations. Nebulae unfold in distant galaxies beyond every telescope that has yet been built. Birdsong fills forests before dawn when no audience is listening. The world is overflowing with beauty that serves no obvious practical purpose.

If creation were concerned only with efficiency, much of this would be unnecessary.

Instead, reality overflows.

This image of God dares to believe that such abundance is not accidental. It reflects a Creator whose generosity exceeds necessity, whose delight spills beyond function, and whose love is not content merely to sustain life but longs to adorn it. Beauty suggests that existence itself is not simply tolerated by God but cherished. We are not cogs in a cosmic machine nor biological accidents struggling to preserve ourselves against extinction. We are beloved participants in a creation that delights in giving more than is required.

Perhaps nowhere is this seen more clearly than in the opening pages of Scripture. Humanity does not awaken in a barren landscape where survival is barely possible. We awaken in a garden. Before there is labor, there is abundance. Before there is striving, there is fruit hanging from trees not planted by human hands. The first experience of humanity is not earning but receiving. We open our eyes to a world already overflowing with gifts.

Beauty continually returns us to that original posture.

It teaches us to receive before we achieve.

To delight before we possess.

To give thanks before we understand.

In doing so, beauty restores something our hurried lives often forget: attention.

The world has not necessarily become less beautiful. We have become less available to it. Our attention is fragmented by endless demands, constant notifications, and the pressure to keep moving. Beauty gently gathers our scattered awareness back into the present moment. It teaches us to linger. To notice. To inhabit rather than merely consume.

This kind of attention changes us.

The philosopher Plato imagined humanity living inside a cave, mistaking shadows cast upon a wall for reality itself. Only by stepping into the light does one discover how small those shadows truly were. Beauty performs a similar miracle. It does not transport us into another world. It reveals this one. The colors become richer. Silence becomes fuller. Ordinary moments begin to shimmer with significance. We realize that what we had taken for the whole of reality was only a dim reflection of something far more radiant.

In that sense, beauty is not an escape from the world but an awakening to it.

It reminds us that heaven is not always found somewhere beyond creation. Sometimes it breaks through creation, making the familiar suddenly luminous. The holy was never absent. It was our attention that had wandered.

This may be the greatest gift of all.

When God is encountered as Beauty, we slowly recover the capacity to live as children rather than machines. We cease relating to life as a sequence of problems to solve and begin receiving it as an inexhaustible gift. Survival, achievement, and understanding all find their proper place, but they are no longer the final goal. Beneath them lies something deeper—a life awakened to wonder.

For perhaps the most beautiful truth this image reveals is that the purpose of life is not merely to endure the world, nor even to master it.

It is to behold it.

And in beholding it, to discover ourselves already surrounded by grace.

### Diapers Are Real

Every image of God reveals something true. Every image also becomes distorted when one beautiful truth is mistaken for the whole of reality. The shadow of beauty does not appear because beauty itself is deceptive. It appears when wonder becomes disconnected from the larger work of love.

Beauty awakens the soul, but it cannot sustain the soul by itself.

When God is imagined primarily through the language of beauty, it is easy to begin believing that the deepest spiritual life is found only in moments of awe. We start to seek the extraordinary—the mountaintop sunrise, the perfect piece of music, the breathtaking cathedral, the retreat that changes everything, the experience that leaves us speechless. Such moments are genuine gifts, but they are never meant to become our permanent home.

Life, after all, is mostly ordinary.

It unfolds through familiar routines, quiet responsibilities, and small acts of faithfulness that rarely feel sublime. Meals must still be prepared. Children still need to be comforted. Bills must be paid. Gardens must be weeded. Friends must be forgiven. Aging parents must be cared for. Work must be done with patience even when it is repetitive and unseen.

The shadow emerges when these ordinary moments begin to feel spiritually inferior simply because they are not beautiful in the way we have learned to recognize beauty.

Without realizing it, we begin chasing experiences rather than inhabiting life. We move from one retreat to another, one concert to another, one inspiring book to the next, hoping to recover the feeling of transcendence that first awakened us. Beauty becomes something to consume rather than something that quietly transforms the way we see the world. We begin collecting moments of wonder while becoming less present to the ordinary miracle unfolding around us every day.

In this way, beauty itself can become another form of striving.

The irony is profound. What first liberated us from the endless pursuit of productivity slowly becomes another pursuit altogether. Instead of measuring our lives by accomplishment, we begin measuring them by intensity. We ask whether we are still inspired, still moved, still captivated. When ordinary days inevitably return, they can begin to feel empty, as though God has somehow withdrawn.

Yet perhaps nothing has changed except our expectations.

The holy has not become less present.

We have simply learned to recognize it only when it arrives dressed in grandeur.

A deeper shadow appears when beauty quietly teaches us to avoid suffering.

Beauty naturally opens us toward delight, harmony, and joy. These are genuine reflections of God. Yet human life also contains illness, grief, failure, loneliness, confusion, and death. There are seasons when the world does not appear beautiful. Hospital rooms rarely feel sublime. Dementia slowly erases familiar faces. Wars leave cities in ruins. Cemeteries are filled with stories interrupted far too soon.

If beauty alone becomes our measure of God's presence, suffering can begin to feel like God's absence.

Yet the larger story of faith refuses that conclusion.

The Scriptures do not end in a garden untouched by sorrow. They pass through exile, wilderness, lament, crucifixion, and countless ordinary acts of endurance. The Christian imagination places its central symbol not in a flower or a sunset but in a cross. By every conventional standard, the cross is ugly. It is an instrument of humiliation, violence, and death. And yet generations of believers have looked upon it and seen a deeper beauty—the beauty of self-giving love that refuses to abandon the world even in its darkest hour.

This image gently reminds us that beauty itself must continue to mature.

At first, we recognize beauty in harmony, brilliance, and delight.

Eventually, we begin to recognize another kind of beauty.

The beauty of forgiveness freely offered.

The beauty of faithful endurance.

The beauty of scarred hands still extended in welcome.

The beauty of compassion that remains present when there is nothing left to admire.

When beauty deepens into love, it becomes large enough to embrace the whole of life.

Another subtle distortion emerges when beauty becomes a way of curating reality rather than receiving it.

The soul slowly develops preferences—not only for beautiful places and works of art, but for beautiful people, beautiful emotions, beautiful communities, and beautiful ideas. We become uncomfortable with awkwardness, unfinishedness, conflict, and disorder. We quietly arrange our lives to maximize inspiration while minimizing interruption.

But love does not curate.

Love receives.

It welcomes the inconvenient guest, the difficult neighbor, the crying child, the cluttered kitchen, the ordinary Tuesday afternoon. It discovers that the divine is not present only in carefully composed moments but also in lives that are untidy, unresolved, and deeply human.

This may be the greatest temptation of all.

Beauty invites us to transcend ourselves, but it can also tempt us to transcend creation itself. We begin longing to escape the ordinary instead of entering it more fully. We seek heaven somewhere beyond the world rather than discovering heaven quietly breaking through within it.

Yet the mystery at the heart of Christianity moves in the opposite direction.

God does not merely reveal himself through dazzling visions or mountaintop experiences. God becomes flesh. The divine enters diapers and dust, carpentry and conversation, shared meals and tired feet, tears and laughter. The Incarnation is a declaration that ordinary human life is not an obstacle to communion with God but its very dwelling place.

The shadow of beauty, then, is not that it loves the world too much.

It is that it sometimes loves only the luminous parts.

The mature soul eventually learns that beauty is not diminished by the ordinary. It is fulfilled there. The sunset and the sink full of dishes belong to the same creation. The symphony and the crying infant both ask for our attention. The cathedral and the hospital room are both places where heaven may become astonishingly near.

For beauty opens our eyes.

But only love teaches us how to keep them open when the light grows dim.

### The Perceiver

A person formed by this image of God becomes, above all else, a person of attention.

While others hurry through the world, they have learned to slow down. While others glance, they behold. They move through life with eyes that remain open to wonder, convinced that the ordinary world is far richer than it first appears. They have discovered that the divine often whispers rather than shouts, revealing itself not only in extraordinary moments but in quiet, everyday gifts that many people have forgotten how to see.

Their greatest gift is not creativity, though many of them become remarkably creative people.

Their greatest gift is perception.

They notice.

They notice the changing light at the end of the day. They notice the silence that settles over a room after difficult news. They notice the lonely person standing unnoticed at the edge of a gathering. They notice the laughter of children, the dignity in an elderly face, the rhythm of rain against a window, the patient unfolding of spring after a long winter. Their lives are shaped by the conviction that attention itself is a form of love.

Some disciples of beauty become artists because they cannot help but give expression to what they have seen.

They become painters, musicians, sculptors, dancers, architects, photographers, poets, filmmakers, chefs, or writers. Yet they rarely understand their work merely as self-expression. They are trying to reveal something. Through color, melody, language, movement, or design, they offer others a glimpse of the beauty that first awakened them. Their work becomes a quiet invitation to behold the world more deeply.

Throughout history, many of the great artists have also been deeply spiritual people, not because they always belonged to organized religion, but because they devoted themselves to seeing. They understood that beauty cannot be manufactured by technique alone. It must first be received. Before one creates beauty, one must learn to recognize it.

Yet the disciple of beauty is not defined by artistic talent.

Some never paint a canvas or compose a symphony.

Instead, they make beautiful lives.

They prepare meals with care, not because anyone is judging them, but because hospitality itself can become an act of reverence. They tend gardens, restore forgotten buildings, arrange flowers, write thoughtful letters, speak words that heal rather than wound, and create spaces where weary people can finally breathe. They understand that beauty belongs as much to the way we welcome another person as it does to masterpieces hanging in museums.

To encounter such a person is often to feel strangely unhurried.

They have little interest in squeezing every moment for maximum productivity. They understand the value of work and gladly give themselves to meaningful labor, but they refuse to let efficiency become the measure of a life. They instinctively know when to pause, when to celebrate, when to laugh, when to sit in silence, and when to allow a conversation to linger long after its practical purpose has ended. Their presence quietly gives others permission to become human again.

The disciple of beauty often possesses something wonderfully childlike.

Not childish, but childlike.

Children are naturally captivated by things adults overlook. They stop to watch ants carrying crumbs across the sidewalk. They collect smooth stones for no reason except delight. They become fascinated by puddles after rain, by butterflies, by clouds, by the sound of wind moving through trees. Many adults slowly lose this capacity as life fills with responsibilities and deadlines.

The disciple of beauty somehow recovers it.

They retain the mature wisdom that comes with age while rediscovering the wonder that often belongs only to children. They remind us that growing older need not mean growing less astonished.

Because they have learned to behold, they also become gentle teachers.

Rarely do they argue people into seeing the world differently. Instead, they point.

"Look."

"Listen."

"Have you noticed?"

Like Jesus drawing attention to lilies in the field, birds in the sky, seeds scattered into the earth, or the joy of a wedding feast, they reveal the extraordinary hidden within the ordinary. They understand that beauty possesses its own quiet authority. Rather than forcing conclusions upon others, they awaken perception. They trust that once someone truly sees, much of the work has already been done.

This way of seeing also gives them a profound sensitivity to suffering.

Because they perceive beauty so deeply, they also feel brokenness with unusual intensity. They grieve scarred landscapes, fractured relationships, abandoned neighborhoods, forgotten people, and needless violence. Ugliness wounds them because they have glimpsed what creation is capable of becoming. Yet they are not naïve romantics. They do not deny the world's pain, nor do they retreat into beautiful experiences to avoid difficult realities.

Instead, they carry beauty into places where beauty seems absent.

They plant gardens in neglected spaces.

They bring music into hospitals.

They preserve old stories and forgotten traditions.

They create homes where strangers become friends.

They speak hope into despair without denying the weight of grief.

They believe that every genuine act of beauty, no matter how small, becomes an act of resistance against despair. It quietly proclaims that darkness does not have the final word.

Perhaps this is why people often leave their presence feeling more alive.

Not because they have been entertained.

Not because they have been convinced.

But because they have awakened.

The disciple of beauty has an uncanny ability to remind others that the world is more radiant than they remembered. A shared meal becomes holy. A walk beneath autumn trees becomes a form of prayer. A piece of music opens the heart to gratitude. An ordinary Tuesday afternoon becomes luminous with meaning simply because someone was fully present to it.

Over time, this disciple becomes less concerned with experiencing beautiful moments and more concerned with becoming a beautiful presence.

The beauty they once sought in sunsets, symphonies, paintings, and mountain vistas slowly takes root within their own lives. Their kindness becomes beautiful. Their patience becomes beautiful. Their humility, hospitality, craftsmanship, attentiveness, and compassion become beautiful. Without trying to draw attention to themselves, they become people through whom others catch glimpses of another world.

They live with the quiet confidence that heaven is never as far away as it seems.

It continually presses against the fabric of ordinary life, waiting to be noticed.

And so the disciple of beauty walks through the world with open eyes and an open heart, discovering again and again that every beautiful thing is an invitation—not to stop with the gift, but to follow its radiance until it disappears into the inexhaustible Beauty from which all beauty comes.

### Music is all Around Us

If this image of God feels like home to you, cherish it. The world needs people who still know how to wonder.

In an age captivated by speed, efficiency, and measurable outcomes, your attentiveness is a quiet act of faith. You remind us that life is more than a sequence of tasks to complete or problems to solve. You remind us to slow our hurried pace, to lift our eyes from our devices, to notice the changing light at the end of the day, to hear birdsong before the noise of the world drowns it out, to linger over meals and conversations instead of rushing toward whatever comes next. You bear witness to something our culture easily forgets: not everything precious can be measured, monetized, or made useful.

Do not lose that gift.

The ability to be astonished is one of the soul's greatest treasures.

Yet remember that beauty has never asked to become your destination.

It has always longed to become your guide.

Every beautiful thing has been pointing beyond itself from the very beginning. The flower does not ask you to remain forever in the garden. The symphony is not diminished when its final note gives way to silence. The mountain does not exist so that you might climb it again and again, but so that you might descend into the valley seeing the whole world with new eyes. Beauty awakens the heart, but its deepest purpose is to teach the heart how to remain awake.

Keep following where beauty leads.

At first, beauty will captivate your senses. You will find it in music, paintings, forests, oceans, cathedrals, poetry, and starlit skies. These gifts are real, and they deserve your gratitude. Receive them fully. Let them interrupt your hurry and restore your capacity for wonder. Let them remind you that creation is not merely functional but extravagantly generous, overflowing with gifts that no practical calculation can ever justify.

But do not stop there.

There is another kind of beauty waiting for you.

Over time, your eyes will begin to change. You will discover that beauty is not confined to moments of obvious splendor. It quietly hides itself in places that the world rarely celebrates. There is beauty in patient craftsmanship that no one applauds. Beauty in friendships that endure across decades. Beauty in marriages that continue choosing one another long after romance has become ordinary faithfulness. Beauty in hands weathered by years of serving others. Beauty in communities that quietly care for the forgotten without seeking recognition.

These forms of beauty are less dramatic.

They are also deeper.

As your vision matures, you may find yourself becoming less captivated by what dazzles and more captivated by what endures. The spectacular slowly gives way to the steadfast. You begin to recognize that beauty is not only something we admire but something we become. A gentle spirit possesses its own beauty. So does humility. So does forgiveness. So does courage that quietly remains when fear would be easier.

Eventually, beauty itself begins to take on another face.

There will come seasons when life no longer feels radiant.

The colors fade.

Music no longer stirs your heart as it once did.

Prayer becomes quiet.

Inspiration retreats beyond the horizon.

Grief enters the room and refuses to leave.

Do not imagine that Beauty has abandoned you.

Some of the deepest forms of beauty can only be seen through tears.

There is beauty in the daughter who patiently feeds her father as dementia slowly steals his memory. There is beauty in the nurse who sits beside the dying long after her shift has ended. There is beauty in the neighbor who faithfully brings meals to a grieving family, expecting nothing in return. There is beauty in the person who keeps showing up, keeps forgiving, keeps loving, long after every feeling of inspiration has disappeared.

This beauty is quieter than a sunset.

It is also more enduring.

If the beauty of creation reveals the generosity of God, the beauty of self-giving love reveals the very heart of God.

Perhaps this is where beauty finally fulfills its purpose.

It teaches you to recognize heaven not only when it bursts into the world through moments of awe, but also when it quietly inhabits the ordinary. The holy is found not only in cathedrals but around kitchen tables. Not only in symphonies but in lullabies sung to restless children. Not only in mountaintop vistas but in hospital rooms where hope refuses to surrender. The same radiance that first captivated you in forests and oceans begins to shine through compassion, patience, sacrifice, and steadfast love.

Do not be surprised if, in time, the people you find most beautiful are no longer the most gifted, accomplished, or celebrated.

Instead, they will be those whose lives have become transparent to love.

The grandmother whose face is lined with kindness.

The teacher who awakens wonder in generations of children.

The craftsman who pours quiet excellence into work few people will notice.

The friend who listens without rushing to speak.

The stranger whose simple act of generosity restores another person's hope.

These people possess a beauty that cannot be manufactured because it has been shaped slowly through years of faithful living.

Follow beauty until it leads you there.

For beauty has never desired your admiration nearly as much as your transformation. It first taught you how to behold the world. Now it longs to teach you how to bless it. What once drew your eyes outward slowly begins to take root within your own soul. Wonder becomes gratitude. Gratitude becomes generosity. Generosity becomes love.

And perhaps that was beauty's purpose all along.

Not simply to fill your life with breathtaking moments, but to awaken in you a way of seeing that never again mistakes the world for something ordinary. To teach you that heaven has always been nearer than you imagined, quietly shimmering through every corner of creation, waiting for eyes that have learned to behold.

Follow every beautiful thing as far as it will take you.

If you do, you may one day discover that beauty was never the end of the journey.

It was the path by which Love first called your name.
