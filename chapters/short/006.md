# Chapter 6

## The God of Control

### Lines and Edges

There is an image of God that emerges wherever the human heart grows weary of uncertainty.

It is born where questions linger unanswered, where plans unravel despite our best efforts, where illness arrives without invitation, where storms gather without warning, and where the future refuses to reveal itself. Faced with such a world, the soul begins to hope that beneath all the apparent disorder there exists a deeper order, a hidden wisdom that has never lost hold of the whole. In this portrait, God is the one who governs what we cannot, sees what we cannot see, and holds together what would otherwise seem to fall apart.

Reality, seen through this image, is not a collection of isolated accidents. Beneath the visible currents of history flows an unseen river of purpose. What appears as coincidence may conceal providence. What feels like interruption may become preparation. Even suffering, though never desired for its own sake, is believed to rest within a wisdom capable of bringing life from places where we can perceive only loss.

God is not imagined as reacting to events as they arise, scrambling to repair what has gone wrong. God appears as the master architect who holds the whole design at once. We live inside individual rooms of the house, seeing only the walls immediately around us. God alone sees the completed dwelling, every hallway, every window, every hidden beam that quietly supports the structure.

This vision has comforted countless generations because it answers one of the oldest fears carried by the human heart: the fear that life is ultimately governed by chaos. We long to believe that history is not drifting aimlessly through darkness, that injustice will not have the final word, that grief is not merely meaningless pain, and that the fragile thread of our lives has not slipped unnoticed into an indifferent universe.

The image often becomes most compelling when life becomes least controllable. A diagnosis interrupts ordinary days. A marriage begins to fracture. A child makes choices no parent can prevent. Financial security disappears. The mind itself becomes a place of uncertainty. In such moments, explanations seldom satisfy the soul. What we seek is not information so much as assurance. We long to know that someone wiser than ourselves has not abandoned the story.

There is something profoundly compassionate about this longing. It is not simply a desire for power. More often it is the cry of creatures who know how small they are. Human beings awaken each morning into a world they did not create, sustained by countless forces they neither understand nor command. The sun rises without our permission. Our hearts beat without our instruction. Breath itself arrives as gift before it ever becomes choice.

This image whispers that beneath every visible uncertainty there remains an invisible faithfulness. Kingdoms rise and fall. Economies flourish and collapse. Our own plans succeed one day and fail the next. Yet the deeper currents of reality remain steady because they are held within the wisdom of One who neither panics nor forgets.

The world may tremble, but its foundation does not.

If the universe is governed by wisdom rather than accident, then every moment becomes worthy of attention. Nothing is merely ordinary. Every encounter, every delay, every unexpected turn may carry significance beyond what first appears. Life is no longer experienced as a random succession of events, but as participation in a story whose Author sees farther than any of its characters.

This portrait reveals something beautiful about the mystery of God. It reminds us that the universe is not ultimately sustained by our intelligence, our vigilance, or our ability to foresee what lies ahead. There is a difference between the limits of our understanding and the limits of reality itself.

We need not believe that everything is understandable in order to believe that everything is held.

Long before we learn to loosen our own grasp upon life, we are invited to imagine a God whose grasp has never loosened upon us.

### Stick to the Plan

When God is seen through this image, worship becomes a way of inhabiting the world. If reality is governed by wisdom rather than chaos, then the faithful life seeks to participate in that wisdom. Devotion takes the shape of attentiveness. One learns to think carefully, act deliberately, and steward faithfully what has been placed into one’s hands.

Responsibility is not viewed as a burden imposed from above but as a sacred calling. Human beings are not passive spectators watching providence unfold from a distance. They cultivate gardens, raise children, build communities, heal the sick, seek justice, and bring beauty where neglect once reigned. Worship appears in calendars faithfully kept, promises honored, work completed with integrity, and ordinary duties carried out with love.

This vision naturally cultivates habits that give shape to life. Decisions matter because tomorrow is built, in part, from the choices of today. Prudence becomes an act of compassion. Planning becomes an expression of care. Preparation becomes a form of love for those who will one day depend upon us. The person who repairs the roof before the storm is not merely being practical. They are honoring a world in which consequences are real.

There is something deeply beautiful in this form of worship. It refuses to divide the sacred from the ordinary. Washing dishes, paying bills, tending one’s health, finishing difficult work, and caring for aging parents all become holy ground. If God is a God of order, then bringing a little more order into one’s corner of creation becomes a quiet participation in the divine life itself.

This image also inspires the search for wisdom. One studies Scripture, observes creation, seeks counsel, and reflects before acting. Every truth discovered is received as another glimpse into the wisdom by which God sustains the world. Understanding becomes reverence.

Such worship produces dependable friends, thoughtful leaders, careful craftsmen, wise parents, and conscientious neighbors. It resists drifting through life as though little mattered. It teaches the worshiper to ask not merely, *What do I want?* but, *What is wise? What is faithful? What best serves the good of others?*

Yet beneath this beautiful devotion, another movement can quietly emerge. Stewardship begins to blur into management. Faithfulness becomes entangled with outcomes. Planning, once an expression of love, begins to promise safety. Preparation becomes an attempt to eliminate uncertainty. The worshiper no longer seeks wisdom simply to be faithful, but to ensure that life unfolds as it should.

Prayer itself can become caught in this tension. It begins as surrender, the quiet confession that our lives rest in hands wiser than our own. But gradually it can become a search for certainty. The worshiper looks for signs, confirmations, and hidden blueprints that will guarantee the right decision. Every crossroads begins to feel charged with enormous consequence, as though one mistaken step could unravel the entire story.

This impulse is rarely born from pride. More often it grows from love mingled with fear. Parents long to protect their children. Spouses long to preserve their marriages. Leaders hope to spare others unnecessary suffering. Beneath careful planning often lives a tender heart that has learned, perhaps through painful experience, how devastating disorder can be.

For those who have endured profound chaos, this form of worship can become especially compelling. Someone raised in an unpredictable home may cling to structure because structure once meant survival. A person who has suffered financial collapse may become meticulous with every dollar. Another who has known betrayal or sudden illness may spend years trying to anticipate every possibility. What appears to others as excessive control is often the soul’s attempt to ensure yesterday’s wounds are never repeated.

Here the worshiper stands at a quiet crossroads. One path leads toward an ever-tightening grip, where every uncertainty demands another strategy and another contingency. The other leads toward deeper trust. It is the discovery that responsibility is not the same thing as sovereignty, that diligence need not become domination, and that faithful stewardship has limits beyond which only God may go.

The mature worship of this image is peaceful participation. It is rising each morning to do the work love requires while refusing to carry what love never asked us to bear. It is planning carefully without demanding guarantees, acting wisely without imagining that wisdom can remove every risk, and praying earnestly without believing that prayer grants mastery over outcomes.

The open hand becomes as holy as the diligent one.

Worship begins by believing that God is in control of all things. It matures by discovering that we are not. In learning that difference, the soul slowly exchanges the exhausting illusion of mastery for the quiet freedom of trust.

### A Firm Foundation

Every enduring image of God survives because it answers a genuine human need. The image of God as the one who governs, orders, and upholds the world has endured because it speaks to our longing for a universe that is not abandoned to chaos. It tells us that our lives matter, our choices matter, and the world entrusted to us is worthy of care.

Its first gift is stewardship.

When God is imagined as the keeper of order, the world itself begins to appear sacred. Time is not something to spend carelessly but something entrusted to us. Relationships become gardens to cultivate rather than conveniences to consume. Work becomes more than survival; it becomes participation in the ongoing care of creation. Ordinary responsibility is elevated into devotion.

This image calls forth some of humanity’s finest qualities. It teaches diligence without demanding perfection, prudence without extinguishing creativity, and discipline without despising joy. Love is expressed through practical faithfulness: meals prepared before hunger arrives, repairs made before collapse, truth spoken before resentment hardens, and quiet presence offered to the people entrusted to our care. The world becomes more livable because someone believed careful attention mattered.

Civilizations bear witness to this gift. Every bridge carefully engineered, every hospital thoughtfully organized, every school patiently built, and every emergency plan prepared before disaster strikes reflects something of this impulse. Planning is not opposed to faith. Properly understood, it is one of the ways faith expresses itself.

Yet stewardship is not this image’s only gift.

Sooner or later, every human being reaches the edge of their own competence. We encounter losses no preparation could have prevented, problems no intelligence can solve, and sorrows that refuse every attempt at management. The child still becomes ill. The marriage still ends. The diagnosis still arrives. Every careful plan eventually runs out of road.

Here this image offers its deeper gift: surrender.

There is profound freedom in realizing that the universe has never depended entirely upon our strength. Long before we learned to organize our calendars or anticipate tomorrow’s problems, the earth continued turning beneath our feet. Morning still followed night. Seasons still unfolded. Life itself continued to be sustained by a wisdom far older and far greater than our own.

Responsibility belongs to us.

Sovereignty never did.

For anxious souls, this becomes a place of healing. We discover that not every burden belongs upon our shoulders. We may faithfully carry what has been entrusted to us without imagining that we must also carry the future of those we love, the course of history, or every outcome that lies forever beyond our reach. What exceeds our strength does not exceed God’s care.

This insight reshapes our relationship with uncertainty. Instead of seeing uncertainty as evidence that everything is falling apart, we begin to recognize it as the natural boundary between creature and Creator. We are finite not because we have failed, but because we were never meant to be infinite. We cannot foresee every consequence, guarantee every outcome, or heal every wound. This is not a defect in our design. It is the truth of being human.

When this truth takes root, peace begins to emerge. The soul still plans, but no longer believes every plan must succeed. It still works diligently, but no longer measures its worth by outcomes alone. It still prays earnestly, but no longer imagines that prayer is a mechanism for controlling reality. Wisdom consists not in carrying everything, but in discerning what truly belongs within our care.

This image refuses two opposite illusions at once. It refuses the belief that nothing we do matters, and it refuses the belief that everything depends upon us. It invites us into the narrow path between them: faithful participation sustained by humble dependence. We plant and water, knowing that growth remains a mystery beyond our command.

Stewardship and surrender are not competing virtues but companions. One gives dignity to our labor. The other gives freedom to our hearts. Together they reveal one of the deepest truths preserved by this image of God: we honor God most fully not by trying to become sovereign ourselves, but by offering our faithful work into the hands of the One who already is.

### Puppets on Strings

This portrait casts its shadow when one facet of the divine mystery is mistaken for the whole. The God of Control reveals something profoundly true: the world is not abandoned, wisdom is worthy of pursuit, and our choices genuinely matter. Yet when this image becomes absolute, stewardship quietly transforms into something else. The hands meant to tend the garden begin trying to hold the world together.

The first shadow is not domination but fear.

Few people become controlling because they crave power for its own sake. More often, they become controlling because they have known what it feels like when life slips beyond their grasp. A childhood marked by instability, the betrayal of someone trusted, the death of a loved one, financial collapse, or illness without warning can leave a quiet promise buried within the soul:

*Never again. Next time I will be prepared.*

From that promise grows an exhausting vigilance. The mind begins scanning constantly for what might go wrong. Every decision must be analyzed. Every risk weighed. Every conversation rehearsed before it happens. What began as prudence becomes hypervigilance, and the future is experienced less as possibility than as a landscape of threats waiting to be managed.

This shadow often disguises itself as wisdom. The person appears responsible, organized, and conscientious. Others admire their discipline, unaware of the anxiety quietly driving it. Inside, however, life has become a relentless effort to carry what cannot actually be carried. The soul has confused vigilance with peace, preparation with trust, and management with faithfulness.

Control eventually becomes an identity. The worshiper no longer practices responsibility; they begin feeling responsible for everything. They carry the emotional climate of every relationship, the happiness of those they love, the success of every project, the future of their children, and sometimes even the spiritual lives of others. The boundaries between their calling and another person’s freedom slowly disappear. Love becomes over-functioning.

This same image can also produce the opposite distortion. If God governs every detail of history, one may quietly wonder whether human effort matters at all. Waiting upon God becomes avoiding difficult decisions. Trust becomes passivity. Instead of grasping too tightly, the person withdraws from responsibility altogether.

Though these responses appear opposite, they share the same root. Neither knows how to live peacefully within uncertainty. One tries to eliminate uncertainty through relentless effort. The other escapes it by refusing to act. Both confuse the roles of Creator and creature.

This is the deepest shadow of the image: the confusion of stewardship with sovereignty.

The worshiper begins occupying responsibilities that belong only to God while neglecting the simple responsibilities that actually belong to them. They mistake omniscience for wisdom, believing enough information will eliminate mystery. They mistake omnipotence for diligence, believing enough effort will remove vulnerability. Without realizing it, they begin trying to live as though they possess attributes that belong to God alone.

The consequences spill into every relationship. Children feel managed rather than guided. Friends feel corrected rather than accompanied. Colleagues experience micromanagement where collaboration was intended. Even prayer subtly changes its character. Instead of becoming a place of surrender, it becomes another strategy for securing the desired outcome.

Perhaps nowhere is this shadow more painful than in suffering. When life refuses to cooperate despite our best efforts, we assume the failure somehow belongs to us.

*If only I had prayed differently. Planned more carefully. Been wiser. Been more faithful.*

Regret becomes a substitute for grief because regret preserves the comforting illusion that control was possible all along.

Wonder also begins to disappear. Life becomes a project to manage rather than a mystery to receive. Surprise is treated as interruption instead of invitation. Spontaneity feels irresponsible. Joy becomes difficult because joy flourishes most naturally in hearts willing to receive life as gift rather than possession. The person becomes so occupied with protecting tomorrow that they quietly lose today.

Still, this shadow does not invalidate the gift from which it grew. The desire to be responsible is beautiful. The longing to protect those we love is beautiful. The impulse to prepare wisely and reduce unnecessary suffering reflects something genuinely holy. The distortion begins only when these good desires are asked to carry more than they were ever meant to bear. Stewardship becomes pseudo-sovereignty. Love becomes management. Wisdom becomes certainty.

Healing is not found in abandoning responsibility, but in rediscovering proportion. Some burdens belong in our hands. Others never did. Faithfulness does not guarantee outcomes. Love does not require omniscience. Wisdom often consists less in controlling the future than in remaining present within its uncertainty.

God may indeed hold all things together.

The danger begins only when we imagine that we must do the same.

### The Builder

We become like what we worship.

A person formed by this image of God often becomes someone others naturally trust. They arrive early, remember the details, and quietly notice what everyone else has overlooked. They carry responsibilities without being asked, keep their promises when it is inconvenient, and remain steady when circumstances become uncertain. In families, workplaces, churches, and communities, they often become the hidden beams that keep the structure standing.

Their faith is rarely loud. It is expressed through reliability.

To this disciple, love has weight. Caring for another person means more than feeling affection; it means assuming responsibility. Love prepares meals before hunger arrives, checks on aging parents, saves for difficult seasons, studies before offering advice, and repairs what is broken before someone is harmed by its neglect. Their devotion is practical. It is measured in calendars, budgets, careful plans, and promises faithfully kept.

They are often remarkably capable. They know how to solve problems, organize complexity, and remain calm while others panic. They think several steps ahead, anticipating consequences before they unfold. Others look to them for guidance because their competence feels like shelter.

Yet beneath this strength there is often a quieter story.

Many disciples of this image did not become responsible because responsibility appealed to them. They became responsible because, somewhere along the way, life taught them that someone had to be. Perhaps they grew up where stability was fragile. Perhaps they lived through betrayal, illness, financial hardship, or seasons when the adults themselves seemed unable to hold the family together. They discovered early that vigilance felt safer than trust and preparation felt wiser than hope.

Responsibility became more than a virtue.

It became a way of surviving.

As the years pass, this posture shapes the soul. The disciple instinctively scans for problems before they appear. They notice tensions in conversations, weaknesses in plans, risks in decisions, and needs in other people long before anyone else does. This attentiveness can be an extraordinary gift, but it comes at a cost. A mind that is always watching rarely discovers how to rest.

Because they are so often the competent one, they may begin believing that being needed is the same thing as being loved. Their value becomes intertwined with usefulness. Rest feels selfish. Asking for help feels like failure. Their gifts remain genuine, but those gifts slowly become the foundation upon which their identity is built.

Then life does what life inevitably does.

A child chooses a path they cannot redirect. A marriage suffers despite years of careful effort. A trusted friend walks away. A diagnosis arrives that no preparation could have prevented. Suddenly the disciple encounters a reality that refuses every strategy they have spent a lifetime perfecting.

It is often here that this image reveals its deepest wisdom.

The disciple slowly discovers that dependence is not the opposite of maturity, but one of its highest expressions. Love does not require omnipotence. Presence is often more healing than solutions. Faithfulness is measured not by perfect outcomes, but by wholehearted participation in what has actually been entrusted to them. The illusion of control begins to loosen, not because life has become more predictable, but because the soul has become more honest about its limits.

This transformation is usually quiet rather than dramatic. The disciple still plans, but without demanding certainty. They still prepare, but no longer imagine preparation can eliminate every risk. They still work diligently, yet they no longer confuse their labor with the source of the harvest. Instead of asking, *How can I make sure everything turns out well?* they begin asking, *What does love require of me today?*

This freedom changes the way they relate to others. They allow children to make mistakes that foster wisdom. They permit friends to carry their own responsibilities rather than rescuing them from every consequence. They offer counsel without demanding compliance. Compassion becomes gentler because it no longer needs to direct every outcome.

Perhaps most importantly, they become gentler with themselves. They begin to forgive the younger self who believed the world depended upon constant vigilance. They recognize that many of their controlling instincts were not signs of arrogance but attempts to love within a frightening world. What once served as armor is no longer needed in quite the same way.

The mature disciple remains conscientious, disciplined, and dependable. They still value wisdom, preparation, and responsibility because these remain genuine expressions of love. But they have discovered something greater.

The world does not ask them to be God.

It asks them only to be faithful.

Their strength becomes softer. Their diligence becomes lighter. Their faith becomes spacious enough to include mystery. They build without possessing, serve without dominating, plan without clinging, and love without imagining that love requires control. They faithfully tend the small corner of creation entrusted to their care, trusting that the rest has always belonged in the hands of God.

### An Open Hand

There is a quiet irony hidden within this image of God. We are drawn to the God of Control because we long for peace, yet so often we seek that peace by becoming more controlling ourselves. We mistake vigilance for trust, management for wisdom, and certainty for faith. The very image that promises to free us from anxiety can, if held too tightly, teach us to carry burdens we were never meant to bear.

And yet this image preserves a truth the soul desperately needs. We are not wandering through an abandoned universe. Our lives are not suspended over chaos by the fragile thread of our own competence. Beneath all that we can foresee and all that remains hidden, there is a faithfulness that does not depend upon our ability to understand it.

Before we ever learned to hold on, we were already being held.

Perhaps that is the invitation this image offers: not to become masters of providence, but students of it. To participate wholeheartedly in the work entrusted to us while releasing the need to guarantee its outcome. Responsibility and surrender are not enemies. One calls us to faithful action. The other frees us from the illusion that everything depends upon us.

It is a difficult lesson because it asks us to relinquish one of our deepest illusions: that if we could simply think clearly enough, pray faithfully enough, plan carefully enough, or love intensely enough, we might finally secure the future against loss. Few illusions are more comforting. Few are more exhausting. Life, sooner or later, invites us beyond that illusion—not into despair, but into dependence.

As you leave this chapter, it may be worth asking where this image has shaped your own life.

When has it given you courage to act wisely, to persevere, to prepare, and to care faithfully for what has been entrusted to you? When has it taught you that love is often expressed through ordinary acts of responsibility? And when has that same longing for order quietly become anxiety, perfectionism, over-functioning, or the inability to rest?

Perhaps you will discover that this image lives in you more deeply than you first imagined. It may appear in the plans you make, the responsibilities you assume, the scenarios you rehearse before falling asleep, or the quiet belief that everything depends, somehow, upon your ability to keep life from unraveling.

If so, meet that part of yourself gently.

It is likely driven less by pride than by love, by old wounds, or by the deep desire to protect what is precious.

The invitation is not to abandon responsibility, but to rediscover its proper home. Continue to build. Continue to prepare. Continue to keep your promises, tend your relationships, and care for the small corner of creation entrusted to you. But remember that your hands, however faithful, were never meant to become the hands that uphold the world.

The God who calls us to stewardship is also the God who continually invites us to open our clenched fists. The One who teaches us to labor with diligence also whispers, again and again,

*You may rest.*

For we become like what we worship.

May this image teach us to become responsible without becoming burdened, diligent without becoming anxious, and faithful without imagining that faithfulness requires control. May it form in us the wisdom to know what belongs in our hands and what belongs in God’s. And when our own strength reaches its limits, may we discover the quiet freedom of an open hand, trusting that the One who has always held the world has never ceased holding us as well.
