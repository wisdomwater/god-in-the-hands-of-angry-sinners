# Chapter 2

## We All Have Gods

The title of this book may strike some readers as unusual.

Perhaps you consider yourself religious. Perhaps you do not. You may identify as Christian, Jewish, Muslim, Hindu, Buddhist, spiritual but not religious, agnostic, atheist, or something else entirely. Whatever label you wear, you may be tempted to think that a discussion about God applies only to people who belong to formal religious traditions.

I would like to suggest otherwise.

My suspicion is that every human being worships. Not necessarily through prayers, hymns, rituals, or sacred texts, but through the simple act of organizing life around what matters most. The religious worship, and so do the secular. The spiritually minded worship, and so do materialists. The scientist, the activist, the entrepreneur, the artist, and the mystic all orient themselves around a vision of what is good, true, beautiful, or worth pursuing.

The question, then, is not whether we worship. The question is what we worship.

At this point, some readers may object. Surely an atheist does not worship anything. After all, worship seems inseparable from belief in a deity.

That is certainly true in one sense. Yet I am using the word in a broader way. Every human life revolves around something. Every person possesses a set of values that shape their decisions, inform their priorities, and give direction to their actions. Whether consciously or unconsciously, we all answer questions such as: What is most important? What is worth sacrificing for? What makes life meaningful? What should guide my choices?

The answers differ from person to person, but the existence of those answers is nearly universal.

For some, the highest value is found in a personal God. For others, it may be reason, freedom, justice, family, nation, wealth, pleasure, human flourishing, or scientific understanding. Some place their trust in divine revelation. Others place it in human progress. Some find meaning in spiritual awakening, while others find it in the advancement of knowledge.

The forms vary considerably, yet they serve a similar function. Each becomes a compass by which a life is navigated. Each offers a vision of reality and a sense of what matters most. Each quietly shapes the person who follows it.

When I use the word *God* throughout this book, I am not always referring to a supernatural being seated somewhere beyond the stars. Rather, I am referring to that which functions as ultimate in a person's life. The highest value. The deepest loyalty. The vision around which everything else is organized.

In that sense, we all have gods.

This brings us to a second question.

What does it mean to worship?

For many of us, worship immediately evokes religious imagery. We picture congregations singing hymns, kneeling in prayer, reciting creeds, or gathering in sacred spaces. These are certainly forms of worship, but they are not its essence.

At its heart, worship is a matter of attention.

We become shaped by whatever consistently occupies our awareness. We return to certain ideas again and again. We rehearse them in our minds. We measure our lives against them. We make decisions in light of them. Over time, they begin to influence not only what we think, but who we are becoming.

A person who spends years contemplating wealth may eventually view the world through the lens of wealth. Relationships become investments. Time becomes a resource to be optimized. Success becomes a measure of personal worth. This transformation rarely happens all at once. It emerges gradually, almost invisibly, through countless acts of attention.

The same can be said of power. The same can be said of beauty, security, achievement, justice, or compassion. Whatever repeatedly captures our imagination begins to leave its imprint upon us.

The ancient spiritual traditions understood this well. They placed enormous emphasis on practices such as prayer, meditation, contemplation, remembrance, and devotion because they recognized a profound truth about human nature: we become what we consistently attend to.

Modern psychology often describes this process in different language. Habits shape behavior. Repeated thoughts strengthen neural pathways. Attention influences perception. Character is formed through countless small choices repeated over time. Though the vocabulary has changed, the insight remains remarkably similar.

The object of our devotion slowly forms the architecture of the soul.

This is why our images of God matter.

Whether we consciously realize it or not, our image of the divine becomes a model for human flourishing. It teaches us what to admire, what to fear, what to pursue, and what to avoid. It shapes our assumptions about justice, mercy, power, forgiveness, belonging, and love.

If God is primarily wrathful, we may come to regard wrath as virtuous. If God is primarily tribal, we may learn to divide the world into insiders and outsiders. If God is primarily compassionate, compassion begins to appear sacred. If God is primarily restorative, we become more inclined toward healing than condemnation.

The image we contemplate becomes the life we aspire to embody.

This does not mean that every image of God is wholly right or wholly wrong. Nor does it mean that human beings consciously choose the values that shape them. Most of us inherit our images long before we examine them. They come to us through family, culture, religion, education, experience, and personal temperament. We absorb them from the air around us.

Yet sooner or later, each of us is confronted with a question.

What kind of person is this vision producing?

Am I becoming more loving or more fearful? More compassionate or more judgmental? More open or more rigid? More humble or more certain of my own righteousness?

The answer to those questions may reveal as much about our gods as any creed we recite.

This is the great challenge and the great opportunity of the spiritual life. Every image of God carries within it a vision of humanity. Every vision of the highest good contains an invitation. And every invitation, if followed long enough, leaves its mark upon the soul.

The chapters that follow are an exploration of those images. Some will feel familiar. Some may feel uncomfortable. Yet each offers a window into a deeper question that lies at the heart of this book:

What is the highest thing in my life?

And what is it making of me?
