# Chapter 12

## The God of Freedom

### The Open Door

In this image, God is encountered as the Liberator.

Here the divine is understood not primarily as ruler, judge, teacher, or artist, but as the one who opens what has been shut, loosens what has been bound, and calls creation into ever greater freedom. This portrait of God resonates deeply because it speaks to one of the most universal longings of the human heart: the desire to be free. Every generation, every culture, and every person knows something of constraint. We know what it is to feel trapped by fear, burdened by shame, confined by expectation, oppressed by injustice, or imprisoned by habits we no longer wish to carry. To imagine God as Liberator is to believe that the deepest movement of the divine is not toward domination but toward release.

This image has echoed through history in countless forms. It inspired slaves who dreamed of deliverance, reformers who challenged oppressive powers, pioneers who crossed frontiers in search of new beginnings, and activists who refused to accept that injustice was the final word. It lives within revolutions and revivals alike. It pulses beneath declarations of human dignity and cries for liberation. In cultures shaped by the ideals of liberty—perhaps nowhere more visibly than in America—this portrait of God has become woven into the moral imagination. Freedom is not merely a political value but a sacred one, reflecting the intuition that human beings were not created to live beneath unnecessary chains.

The Scriptures give profound voice to this intuition. The defining act of God in Israel's memory is not conquest but liberation. God hears the cries of the enslaved, confronts Pharaoh, parts the sea, and leads a captive people into the wilderness. Again and again, the biblical story returns to this pattern. God releases prisoners. God delivers the oppressed. God breaks yokes, restores exiles, and proclaims liberty to those held captive. Even Jesus announces his ministry in the language of freedom, declaring good news to the poor and release to those in bondage. Throughout the biblical narrative, God appears as the one who continually opens doors that human beings believed would remain forever closed.

Yet this image invites us to ask a deeper question: what is freedom?

Our culture often answers by defining freedom negatively. Freedom is the absence of restraint. It is the ability to choose without interference, to determine one's own course, to live without another imposing their will upon us. There is genuine wisdom in this understanding. Freedom from tyranny, coercion, manipulation, and oppression is a precious good. Every person bears the image of God and therefore deserves to live without being owned, diminished, or controlled by another. Much of humanity's noblest work has been the long struggle to remove these chains.

But the biblical vision quietly presses further. Freedom is never merely freedom *from*. It is also freedom *for*.

Israel is not delivered from Egypt simply so they may wander forever. They are liberated so they can become a people. Their chains are removed not because liberation itself is the destination, but because covenant, communion, and flourishing await on the other side. Likewise, when the Apostle Paul proclaims, "It is for freedom that Christ has set us free," he is not celebrating autonomy as an end in itself. Almost immediately he reminds his readers not to use freedom as an excuse for self-indulgence, but to serve one another through love. Freedom is not diminished by love. It finds its fulfillment there.

This image of God therefore reveals something surprising about the divine nature. God does not simply desire free creatures; God desires creatures capable of love. And love cannot exist without freedom.

Love that is compelled is no longer love. Obedience extracted through fear may produce conformity, but it cannot produce communion. Affection that has no possibility of refusal is not relationship but programming. Love must always retain the possibility of being rejected, or it ceases to be freely given. This may even illuminate one of the oldest questions in theology: Why does God seem hidden? Why does the Creator not overwhelm every doubt with undeniable displays of power?

Perhaps because the God who seeks lovers rather than subjects refuses to overpower the freedom necessary for genuine relationship.

This is not to suggest that divine hiddenness explains every experience of silence or suffering. There remain mysteries no philosophy can dissolve. Yet this portrait invites us to consider that God's restraint may itself be an expression of divine love. God is near enough to be found but not so overwhelming that trust becomes unavoidable. The invitation remains real because the response remains free.

Seen this way, freedom is not merely one of God's gifts. It is something woven into God's own life. God does not create because of necessity but generosity. God forgives because love overflows, not because an external law compels mercy. Every act of grace is freely given. Divine love is never coerced, calculated, or forced. It is the spontaneous expression of perfect freedom.

To be made in the image of such a God is therefore to be created not merely with the ability to choose, but with the capacity to become participants in this same freedom. The goal is not endless independence nor perpetual self-assertion. It is to become so liberated from fear, shame, pride, and compulsion that love may flow without obstruction. Freedom, in its deepest sense, is not the permission to become whatever we desire. It is the restoration of our ability to become what love created us to be.

This portrait of God opens every prison door, but it never pushes us through. It loosens every chain, but it does not drag us into maturity. Instead, the Liberator stands at the threshold with open hands, inviting rather than compelling, calling rather than coercing. In this image, God's greatest display of power is not the ability to control creation, but the willingness to set it free. And perhaps that is because the highest purpose of freedom is not independence at all, but the possibility that love might one day be freely returned.
