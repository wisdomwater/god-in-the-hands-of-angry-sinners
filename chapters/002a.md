# Part I

## Images of Survival

Every spiritual life begins somewhere, and more often than not it begins close to the earth.

Before we ever asked what is beautiful, or what is true, or what love might ask of us, we asked a simpler and more urgent question: *How do we survive?* Before there were doctrines to defend or philosophies to refine, there were winter nights, empty fields, invading armies, and the terrible knowledge that everything we love can be taken from us. Human beings did not first search for God from a place of safety. We searched hungry, afraid, exposed, and unsure whether tomorrow would come.

It should not surprise us, then, that some of our oldest and most powerful images of God were born in that atmosphere. When life feels fragile, God is often imagined in ways that can hold back the dark. God appears as the one who punishes what threatens the community, who draws a boundary around a people, who raises up strong rulers, who defends order against chaos, and who rewards loyalty while warning against betrayal. In these portraits, the divine is encountered not first as mystery or beauty, but as shelter, authority, judgment, and strength.

This does not make these images false.

It makes them human.

They arise from the oldest layers of the soul, from the part of us that knows life can break, that children must be protected, and that communities can dissolve. Before a people can speak of freedom, they must survive conquest. Before the heart can open to mystery, it often longs for shelter—for some assurance that the world is not entirely wild.

So Part I begins here, with what might be called *the images of survival*: the Wrathful God, the Tribal God, and the King God. They are not separate deities competing for allegiance, but recurring ways human beings have imagined the one God where fear and faith meet, where the longing for God is braided together with the longing to be safe.

To understand these images, we must first acknowledge the truth they carry.

They know that evil is real. They know that communities require order, that loyalty matters, and that a people without memory dissolves. They remind us that spiritual life is not lived by disembodied souls floating above history, but by vulnerable creatures who must feed their children, bury their dead, and somehow continue when the world around them is unstable.

There is wisdom in that.

There is even mercy in it.

For what is a wrathful image of God if not the hope that injustice will not have the last word? What is a tribal image of God if not the hope that one's people are not forgotten in the vastness of history? What is a kingly image of God if not the longing for a power strong enough to restrain chaos and hold the world together? These portraits endure because they answer real cries of the human heart. They offer seriousness in a dangerous world, belonging in a lonely one, and order in a world that can so easily come apart.

And yet every image that protects us can also begin to imprison us if it is mistaken for the whole.

A vision of God born in fear may struggle to imagine mercy for the outsider. A portrait shaped by tribal loyalty may find it difficult to bless those beyond the boundary. An image formed around power and protection may teach reverence and obedience, yet leave little room for tenderness, freedom, or wonder. The fortress that kept danger out can also keep love from getting in.

Fear is not the problem. Fear belongs to every human life. The problem comes when fear is given the final word, when a true but partial vision of God becomes so dominant that everything else is pushed to the edges. Judgment eclipses compassion. Loyalty eclipses universality. Order eclipses freedom. Survival, a necessary beginning, quietly becomes the whole story.

But survival is never the whole story.

Still, we should not move too quickly past these images, as though mature spirituality had left them behind. They remain alive in our politics, our churches, our families, and our private anxieties. They rise whenever life feels threatened, whenever we divide the world into insiders and outsiders, or long for someone strong enough to restore order. The ancient images still live in modern hearts.

So we begin here not to mock these images, but to understand them. Every one reveals something real. Every one protects something precious. Every one also leaves something in shadow.

For we become like what we worship.

Before we can speak of freedom, beauty, compassion, or love, we must first pass through the older sanctuaries—the places where human beings first learned to bow before the God they hoped would keep them alive.
